## Option 1 (short, direct)

New post. I've been reading multi-agent LLM systems papers for weeks and wanted a map I wished I'd had on day one. This is Part 1 of a short series that tries to be that map.

The TLDR of Part 1: the MAS field has had two rough waves of research (coordination theory in 2023, reliability work starting in 2025), with one outside disruption between them that reshaped both (the agentic coding turn of 2024). The waves aren't a historical claim. They're a reader aid. Once you see the shape, the thirty-paper reading list becomes navigable.

Eight more posts coming over the next week and a half, covering vocabulary, the canonical papers, what actually breaks, debate and shared state, verification patterns, benchmarks, and open questions.

Part 1: https://christophermeiklejohn.com/ai/agents/mas-series/2026/04/24/mas-series-01-the-landscape.html

## Option 2 (with the distributed systems hook)

Multi-agent LLM systems have a distributed systems problem. I've written about this before. What I hadn't done was map the literature so anyone else could see it.

New series starting today: "Getting Up to Speed on Multi-Agent Systems." Eight posts over the next week and a half.

Part 1 is the landscape: two waves of MAS research, one outside disruption (the agentic coding turn) that reshaped what MAS has to answer for, and a note that the wave framing is a reader aid rather than a historical claim.

Later posts cover the vocabulary, the canonical papers, why MAS actually breaks in practice (MAST, MAS-FIRE, Silo-Bench), the debate literature, verification patterns including Cursor's visual feedback loop, the benchmark gap, and the open questions I keep tripping over.

Written for anyone who's been told to "just use multi-agent" and wants to know what that actually means.

Part 1: https://christophermeiklejohn.com/ai/agents/mas-series/2026/04/24/mas-series-01-the-landscape.html
